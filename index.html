People with disabilities are often overlooked. A simple explanation for this stems from the medical model of disability, which places blame on disabled individuals, deeming them incapable of executing tasks up to par with their able-bodied counterparts. Of course, disabled individuals may need to seek aid when it comes to certain tasks, but those in favor of the medical model stray away from aiding those in need. Instead, they look toward “fixing” the perceived problem, convincing many disabled individuals that something is wrong with them. Many able-bodied individuals fail to acknowledge a quality present in all disabled people: resilience, a trait shown through pushing forward despite facing various obstacles. For example, one of the blind people’s many hurdles is their lack of eyesight. What many able-bodied people fail to recognize are the other obstacles blind people and other disabled folks face, such as accessibility (or lack thereof) and stigma. Even with such difficulties, they still possess a greater vision many overlook: perspective. Each disabled individual can draw from their unique experiences– exploring their ideas in a new, creative way and amending existing ones innovatively. Many able-bodied people cannot say the same. By including disabled voices and perspectives in our everyday lives, work, and studies, we could be moving farther ahead than lots fail to accept. It is time to think of disabilities in a new light. They don’t make certain tasks impossible, they reveal opportunities to approach said tasks differently.
